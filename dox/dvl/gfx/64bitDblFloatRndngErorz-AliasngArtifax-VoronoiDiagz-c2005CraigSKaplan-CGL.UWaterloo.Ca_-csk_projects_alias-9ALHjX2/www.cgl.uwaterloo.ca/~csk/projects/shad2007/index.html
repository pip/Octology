Geometric Sculpture (Shad Valley 2007)

The construction of 3D geometric sculptures is a beautiful topic that lies at the intersection of art and mathematics. I'll introduce some of the math you need to understand the structure of symmetric polyhedra. Then we'll dive in and try our hand at several different techniques and materials for building geometric sculptures. Some of these techniques are almost like puzzles. You can come to sharpen your intuition for 3D geometry, to build a few pieces of art to take home, or to tackle the puzzle of putting these complex objects together.

Shad Valley is a summer program for high school students where they learn about math, science, business and other fun topics. Part of the program involves signing up for week-long workshops on various topics. I was a Shad in 1989 and loved it. Given that Waterloo is World Shad Headquarters, it seemed only natural to contribute back to the program. I chose geometric sculpture as my workshop topic, and six Shads signed up: Becca, Wooram, Gabe, Jina, Seung, and Wendy.

There were many possible art-math topics that would have been fun for Shad Valley, but I chose sculpture because it's hands-on and doesn't require sitting at a computer.

Here's a breakdown of the workshop topics for future reference, together with links to online resources and photos from our experience.

Day 1: Introduction and Zometool

I started with a quick introduction to geometric sculpture and polyhedra, with enough background material to permit me to derive the Platonic and Archimedean solids. As a first step towards building our own sculptures, we constructed many of these simple polyhedra from Zometool. As a grand finale, I then directed the whole group in the very hasty construction of a projected four-dimensional 120-cell. Everyone was very impressed by it!

Day 2: Tensegrity and Compounds

Tensegrity is a classic subject in geometric sculpture. There is some dispute about its origins, but it seems clear that it originated with some combination of insights by Kenneth Snelson and Buckminster Fuller, and was popularized by Fuller. In a tensegrity sculpture, members are either in tension or compression. Rods are used for compression, and thin wires for tension, giving the construction a light, seemingly gravity-defying look. After searching around the web for a while, I settled on a good scheme for building tensegrities. I cut 5/16" dowels into 4-inch lengths and notched the ends with a newly-purchased Dremel (which I had wanted to acquire anyway). A big bag of elastic bands served for tensional elements (mental note for next time: don't give 500 elastic bands to a room full of high-school students). The elastics had a tendency to fly off the rods, but I eventually discovered a good solution for holding them in place. I bought a few feet of latex rubber hose, and cut it into short lengths that fit nice and snugly on the ends of the rods.

With this tensegrity system, we each built a tensegrity "tetrahedron". When properly assembled, the rods fall into a configuration of the long edges of three mutually perpendicular golden rectangles (not coincidentally, the rectangles that define the vertices of an icosahedron). Perhaps I should post step-by-step instructions for building this shape; send me email if you're reading this and I'll consider it. Then we broke into groups and built some larger sculptures, such as the dodecahedron in the photographs.

After that was done, I wanted to use the rest of the day and the beginning of the next day to build compounds, both to see these lovely objects and to do some classical paper polyhedral construction. We built two of my favourites, the compound of four cubes and the compound of five tetrahedra.

Day 3: Compounds and Modular Origami

We began the day by finishing up our compound models. (Overall, our workmanship on the compounds was a bit below par; I couldn't resist returning to the subject the following week and meticulously building another cube compound myself.)

Then we turned to modular origami, in which many small units are composed together, usually making up the edges of a polyhedral model. This is perhaps the cheapest and most easily accessible sculpture technique we tried, since all you really need is a bunch of paper squares. I opted for a few coloured memo pads instead of more expensive origami paper. The origami paper is more expensive, though it has the advantage that it's only coloured on one side, which can give you some attractive contrasts in finished models.

I showed everyone how to make Sonobe units, which we assembled into cubes and spiky icosahedra. Then the more experienced orgamists moved to the 180 degree Penultimate unit and built a dodecahedron.

References

  • Again, it's very easy to find information on modular origami on the web. As always Wikipedia is a decent place to start.
  • There are many variations on the Sonobe unit that produce different appearences when assembled. We used a relatively simple one as shown in these instructions, but see also this one. It's easy to experiment with this unit.
  • We used Jim Plank's 108 degree Penultimate Module for the dodecahedron. It's actually a very clever bit of folding that produces a close approximation of a 108 degree angle. You don't have to start with a 4x3 piece of paper (4x4 works too), though the results are a bit cleaner.

Day 4: Modular Kirigami

On the final day, we came at last to the ultimate test of manual dexterity, geometric intuition, and puzzle-solving ability. The task was to assemble copies of Deep Sea Tango, a sculptural puzzle created by George Hart. The sculpture is in a form that George calls "Modular Kirigami": a set of precut paper elements that interlock without glue or tape. The elements are complex in shape, and would be difficult to cut from paper if it were not for the craft cutter that I used to cut them automatically. Nevertheless, it still took the whole morning to get the sculptures assembled! George tells me that I'm probably the second person ever to assemble one of these sculptures, which means that the Shads were the third through eighth. Nicely done!
